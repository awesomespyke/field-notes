When I left streaming, I thought I was giving something up.
For years, music arrived effortlessly.
Every Friday there were new releases. Recommendations quietly appeared. Artists I had never heard of somehow found their way into my headphones. It was easy to mistake convenience for discovery.
Without the algorithm, the silence felt strange.
How would I know what was new?
I started browsing instead.
Record labels.
Music magazines.
Discogs.
Not because I was looking for something specific. Simply because I wanted to remain in conversation with music.
The experience was messier than I expected.
Most recommendations weren't for me.
Some albums lasted thirty seconds.
Others I didn't understand at all.
Every now and then, though, I'd stop.
Wait... who is this?
That moment felt different.
Not because I had found my next favorite artist.
Because I hadn't known I was looking.
I realized I wasn't trying to recreate the algorithm.
I was trying to recover wandering.
Then something unexpected happened.
Burning CDs stopped being a nostalgic project.
Buying a record stopped being about collecting.
I found myself thinking less about ownership and more about what these objects made possible.
A friend and I decided to build a shared record library.
Not my records.
Not his records.
Ours.
A collection that could move between two homes, gathering stories along the way.
One person discovers an album.
The other says,
"You have to hear this."
A record leaves the shelf.
A table becomes a listening room.
For an hour, music isn't background.
It's the reason two people are in the same place.
I used to think a library was a building full of books.
Working in libraries taught me otherwise.
A library is an agreement.
No one person has to own everything for everyone to have access.
Perhaps a shared record library is the same kind of agreement.
Not about possession.
About participation.
I've started wondering if every passion has a language.
Photography.
Gardening.
Libraries.
Music.
Learning the language isn't just learning the vocabulary.
It's finding people who speak it too.
Maybe that's what I've been searching for all along.
Not a better way to listen.
A way to make room for relationships around a shared love of listening.
The records matter.
The CDs matter.
The equipment matters.
But only because they give the conversation somewhere to begin.
